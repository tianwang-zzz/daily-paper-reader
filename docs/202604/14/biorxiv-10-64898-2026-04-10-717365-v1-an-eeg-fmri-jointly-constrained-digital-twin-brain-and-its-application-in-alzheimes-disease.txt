# Abstract 17 

Current digital twin brain (DTB) models are typically optimized using single-modality functional 18 neuroimaging data, which restricts their ability to simulate brain dynamics across multiple spatiotemporal 19 scales. Here, we bridged this gap by developing a two-stage DTB (TS-DTB) modeling framework jointly 20 constrained by functional magnetic resonance imaging (fMRI) and electroencephalography (EEG) data. 21 Validation in both the healthy young and Alzheimer’s disease (AD) cohorts demonstrated that the TS-DTB 22 model simultaneously captures subject-specific features of multi-scale brain dynamics. In particular, the TS-23 DTB models of AD patients successfully recapitulated spectral-temporal signatures of cognitive decline, 24 mechanistically linking these deficits to excitation-inhibition (E-I) imbalances. By simulating responses to 25 repetitive transcranial magnetic stimulation (rTMS), we further revealed that cognitive recovery in AD 26 patients can be driven by E-I rebalancing via synaptic reconfiguration and background suppression. Overall, 27 these findings underscore the potential of the TS-DTB to advance the mechanistic understanding of the brain 28 and inform personalized digital therapeutics. 29 

Keywords: Digital twin brain, multimodal integration, spatiotemporal dynamics, translational application 30 31

## 1. Introduction 32 

The digital twin brain (DTB) has emerged as a transformative modeling framework for investigating 33 brain dynamics, offering a unique research perspective on brain function, cognitive behaviors, disease 34 mechanisms, and neuromodulation strategies 1–3 . By coupling structure connectivity with biologically 35 grounded dynamical descriptions, the DTB model reproduces rich macroscopic nonlinear dynamics, 36 including oscillations, synchronization, bifurcations, and critical transitions 4,5 . Driven by the clinical demand 37 for precision medicine, recent advances have increasingly prioritized integrating multiscale architectures and 38 multimodal constraints, evolving from static network fitting toward the reconstruction of dynamic brain 39 states 6–8 . To support this evolution, researchers have systematically incorporated biological priors, such as 40 intrinsic hierarchy and structure–function relationships, into these models to constrain the optimization of 41 high-dimensional parameter spaces 8–11 . The ultimate goal is to elucidate the underlying mechanisms of brain 42 function and disease, predict brain responses, and design personalized, model-driven interventions 12–14 .43 Despite these advances, effectively integrating the complementary insights of multimodal neuroimaging 44 remains a fundamental challenge 15 . The fMRI delineates the hemodynamic network topology with superior 45 spatial resolution, whereas EEG reproduces the rapid spatiotemporal dynamics of neuronal activity 16,17 .46 However, existing DTB modeling strategies often rely on single-modality information constraints, forcing a 47 compromise between spatial topology and temporal characteristics fidelity. For instance, models optimized 48 solely for fMRI functional connectivity frequently fail to generate realistic fast oscillations 9,10,18 . Conversely, 49 those tuned exclusively for EEG or magnetoencephalography (MEG) spectral-temporal often compromise 50 the underlying spatial network architecture 6,19 . This spatiotemporal disconnect creates a fundamental blind 51 spot, preventing current models from reproducing the subtle interplay between network and fast oscillatory 52 dynamics 20 . Resolving this multi-scale interaction is a critical prerequisite for realizing the clinical potential 53 of DTB, particularly for deciphering complex pathologies such as Alzheimer’s disease (AD) 21,22 .54 Consequently, while progress has been made in multimodal integration 6,9,23,24 , achieving a unified, 55 individualized DTB modeling framework that reproduces brain dynamics across multiple spatiotemporal 56 scales remains a critical bottleneck. 57 Overcoming this bottleneck requires the rational parameterization of spatially heterogeneous intra-58 regional dynamics and inter-regional coupling. The human brain exhibits profound regional heterogeneity in 59 synaptic architecture and local dynamics, the complexity often oversimplified by homogeneous parameter 60 assumptions in early DTB models 19,23,25 . Capturing this heterogeneity is indispensable for studying 61 neurodegenerative diseases, which are characterized by spatially specific disruptions in local excitation-62 inhibition (E-I) balance 11,26,27 . However, region-specific parameter estimates that deviate from biological 63 realism inevitably lead to severe overfitting and computational intractability. To break this impasse, a robust 64 strategy must biologically constrain local circuit heterogeneity using spatiotemporal priors, while 65 simultaneously employing precise forward modeling to project simulated source activities onto observable 66 hemodynamics and sensor-space EEG signals 28,29 . Such an approach is essential for developing biologically 67 interpretable models that can serve as testbeds for brain research and the theoretical reference for clinical 68 diagnosis and treatment. 69 To address these challenges, we developed a two-stage DTB (TS-DTB) modeling framework jointly 70 constrained by fMRI and EEG data. To systematically evaluate this framework, we conducted comprehensive 71 validations across healthy young and AD cohorts. In the healthy cohort, the individualized TS-DTB models 72 demonstrated superiority over conventional single-modality and homogeneous models by simultaneously 73 reproducing subject-specific, multi-scale brain dynamics. Extending this validation to a pathological regime, 74 the TS-DTB models of AD patients successfully recapitulated the macroscopic spectral-temporal signatures 75

## 2. Results 82 

2.1 System overview 83 

The foundation of the TS-DTB modeling framework is a biophysically grounded generative model 84 designed to simultaneously simulate macroscopic neuroimaging and fast electrophysiological signals (Fig. 85 1a, Section 4.2). Whole-brain dynamics were simulated using structurally coupled Wilson-Cowan oscillators. 86 These local neural populations generated excitatory firing rates, serving as a biophysical proxy for local field 87 potentials 22 , which were subsequently transformed into observable signals via modality-specific forward 88 models. Specifically, the simulated neural activity was projected into BOLD signals using the Balloon-89 Windkessel model. Concurrently, to capture sensor-space EEG signatures, the source-level electrical activity 90 was mapped to the scalp using individualized leadfields derived from subject-specific EEG forward models, 91 thereby ensuring spatial fidelity. 92 To accurately parameterize this high-dimensional dynamic system, we implemented a two-stage 93 optimization strategy utilizing empirical resting-state multimodal features as benchmarks (Fig. 1b, Section 94 4.3, Tables S1-S3). Recognizing that structural connectivity derived from white matter tractography often 95 imperfectly captures inter-hemispheric communication 8,9 , the first stage refined the source-space effective 96 connectivity (EC) constrained by empirical fMRI functional connectivity (FC) 8. Building upon this optimized 97 network backbone, the second stage focused on reproducing fast sensor-space dynamics by tuning 98 heterogeneous local kinetic parameters. Crucially, this stage leveraged macroscopic fMRI-based gradients as 99 spatial priors. This approach biologically constrains the distribution of regional synaptic strengths, 100 background inputs, and noise to a low-dimensional manifold governed by global gradient weights 11,26 .101 Subsequently, we employed Bayesian optimization to identify the optimal configuration of these weights, 102 minimizing the relative error (RE) against a comprehensive suite of individualized empirical EEG spectral-103 temporal features. By synergizing large-scale network calibration with precise local electrophysiological 104 tuning, the TS-DTB ensures that simulated neural activity authentically reconstructs multiscale brain states. 105 Guided by this system architecture, we systematically evaluated the TS-DTB through comprehensive 106 validation across healthy and AD cohorts (Fig. 1c). In the healthy cohort, we established its methodological 107 robustness by demonstrating that the individualized TS-DTB models exhibit superiority over conventional 108 single-modality models, homogeneous and null baselines (Section 2.2), as well as models lacking 109 individualized EEG forward mapping (Section 2.3), in accurately reproducing subject-specific 110 spatiotemporal dynamics. Transitioning to the AD cohort, we utilized these models to map macroscopic 111 spectral-temporal abnormalities to underlying disruptions in local circuit parameters (Section 2.4). Finally, 112 we deployed the TS-DTB models as an in-silico testbed to simulate rTMS interventions, aiming to track the 113 spatiotemporal responses and decipher the plasticity mechanisms driving cognitive restoration (Section 2.5). 114

2.3 The fidelity of brain dynamics across atlases and EEG forward models 195 

While the synergistic benefits of multimodal constraints were evident, it is imperative to ensure that 196 these improvements are robust against the choice of brain atlases. To verify that the efficacy of the TS-DTB, 197 we assessed its generalization capability using the fine-grained Brainnetome 246 atlas (incorporating 198 subcortical structures) and the high-resolution Schaefer 500 cortical parcellation. Under the Brainnetome 199 parcellation, the He TS-DTB models demonstrated superior optimization performance, achieving significantly 200 lower minimum loss function values (0.705 ± 0.199 vs. 0.789 ± 0.184, p < 0.001; Fig. 4a) and a substantial 201 reduction in REs for spectral features (0.167 ± 0.050 vs. 0.340 ± 0.212, p < 0.001) compared to the Ho models. 202 This superior fidelity in reconstructing empirical spectral and complex time-series features was consistently 203 replicated under the finer-grained Schaefer 500 scheme (Fig. 4e, f, h). Most importantly, the mechanistic 204 negative correlation between the simulated E-I ratio and the 1/f aperiodic exponent remained highly robust 205 across both the Brainnetome 246 ( r=−0.323) and Schaefer 500 ( r=−0.296) atlases (Fig. 4c, g). In contrast, 206 the Ho models showed negligible associations in both spatial resolutions. These findings confirm that 207 gradient-informed heterogeneity captures invariant biological principles of circuit organization that are 208 completely independent of parcellation granularity or anatomical definitions. 209 Beyond brain parcellation, accurate mapping from source-level neural activity to sensor-level signals is 210 a prerequisite for reliable EEG dynamic reconstruction. We quantified this by comparing models utilizing 211 individualized leadfields derived from subject-specific structural MRI against a control scheme using a 212 standardized MNI template. While global optimization losses appeared comparable, individualized forward 213 modeling substantially reduced the RE in the spectral domain (0.199 ± 0.154 vs. 0.290 ± 0.267; Fig. 5a). A 214 more granular analysis revealed that the personalized approach provided a significantly more refined 215 reconstruction of the spectral profile, particularly in capturing the alpha peak and high-frequency energy 216 distributions (Fig. 5b). This spatial fidelity was further corroborated by feature-wise analyses, where 217 individualized modeling consistently minimized REs across spectral-temporal metrics (Fig. 5c). 218 In terms of global network complexity, the standardized model frequently failed to match empirical 219 levels of metastability and synchrony, exhibiting significant statistical deviations across multiple frequency 220 bands (Fig. 5d). Conversely, the individualized models accurately reproduced these nonlinear dynamic 221 markers, indicating that accurate geometric constraints are essential for simulating coordinated network 222 fluctuations. Finally, we examined the reconstruction of large-scale temporal dynamics through EEG 223 microstate analysis. 2.4 Applied to the AD cohort for deciphering E-I imbalance and spectral slowing 251 

Having established an optimal pipeline devoid of age-related physiological drift, we further validated 252 the TS-DTB to an independent AD cohort (Tables S5-S6) to assess its translational capacity (Fig. 6). The 253 models exhibited high fitting accuracy across all groups, including healthy controls, pre-treatment, and post-254 treatment AD patients, maintaining consistently low REs across a comprehensive suite of temporal statistics, 255 entropy, and spectral features (Fig. 6b, e). Crucially, the TS-DTB models successfully recapitulated 256 macroscopic signatures of cognitive states. It accurately replicated the global explained variance and 257 transition probabilities of empirical EEG microstates (Fig. 6c), and closely matched the empirical 258 characteristics of Lempel-Ziv complexity (LZC). Linear fits for LZC yielded highly significant correlations 259 (r=0.837, R² =0.701; r=0.789, R² =0.622; and r=0.882, R² =0.777; all p < 0.001), successfully reproducing the 260 abnormally elevated LZC observed in pre-treatment AD patients compared to controls, as well as the trend 261 of complexity restoration following neuromodulation (Fig. 6d). ## 3. Discussion 362 

In this study, we developed the TS-DTB modeling framework to address the fundamental challenge of 363 reconciling disparate spatiotemporal scales in individualized brain dynamics modeling. By implementing a 364 source-to-sensor optimization strategy, the TS-DTB effectively integrates individualized hemodynamic and 365 electrophysiological constraints, resolving the parameter coordination inherent in single-modality 366

Our validation across both healthy and AD cohorts demonstrated that the generated TS-DTB 367 models surpass conventional optimization schemes by simultaneously reconstructing large-scale network 368 topologies and reproducing fast spatiotemporal dynamics 6,22,23 . Specifically, evaluating the framework in a 369 healthy cohort established its general modeling superiority. More importantly, applying it to the AD cohort 370 allowed us to successfully dissect the microscopic neurophysiological mechanisms underlying macroscopic 371 cognitive decline and to in-silico track therapeutic restoration. 372 The capability to dissect these nuanced spatiotemporal mechanisms relies fundamentally on the holistic 373 integration of multimodal data feature constraints, combining fMRI-derived topology, gradient-informed 374 heterogeneity, and individualized EEG forward modeling. Without these constraints, traditional 375 homogeneous models fail to capture the hierarchical complexity of brain plasticity, erroneously collapsing 376 region-specific dynamic responses into a uniform profile 11,27,44,45 . By constraining local circuit heterogeneity, 377 our approach effectively resolves parameter coordination, enabling the precise estimation of region-specific 378 synaptic strengths and background-related parameters. This biophysically grounded fusion leverages the high 379 spatial resolution of fMRI to disentangle the underlying source-level interactions that drive observable 380 sensor-space oscillations. Furthermore, the incorporation of individualized FEM-based leadfields proved 381 indispensable for resolving the spatial ambiguity of EEG sources, ensuring that modeled cortical dynamics 382 accurately project to real-world sensor observations 28 . This multimodal information jointly constrained 383 strategy differs from previous approaches, offering an effective pipeline for reconciling hemodynamic 384 topology with electrophysiological kinetics. 385 Applying this unified framework to the AD cohort, our model elucidates that the macroscopic spectral 386 slowing and abnormal complexity characteristic of the disease are rooted in a profound microscopic 387 disruption of the local E-I balance 20,40 . Specifically, parameter space analysis in pre-treatment AD patients 388 revealed a gradient-dependent E-I imbalance dominated by aberrant background suppression, which most 389 severely afflicts low-gradient regions. This aligns robustly with the synaptic failure hypothesis, suggesting 390 that as amyloid pathology compromises intrinsic connectivity, neural circuits become increasingly 391 susceptible to random fluctuations, leading to functional network fragmentation and the dissolution of 392 metastable microstates 32,38 . Similarly, consistent E-I imbalance patterns were also observed in an independent 393 MCI cohort, reinforcing the generalizability of our framework 46 . By directly mapping and linking 394 macroscopic EEG abnormalities to specific microcircuit parameters, the TS-DTB models establish the E-I 395 ratio as a mechanistic biomarker that correlates with cognitive performance. This capacity to inversely map 396 sensor-level phenotypes to source-level dysfunction provides a critical theoretical platform for dissecting the 397 neural basis of cognitive decline and restoration and the design of future neuromodulation strategies. 398 Another contribution of this work lies in leveraging the TS-DTB as an in-silico testbed to 399 mechanistically dissect rTMS-induced cognitive restoration. Challenging the conventional assumption that 400 therapeutic plasticity is strictly confined to the stimulation target, our analysis revealed a sophisticated, 401 gradient-dependent strategy of E-I rebalancing characterized by a global synergy of peripheral drive and core 402 homeostasis 42,43 . Specifically, we observed that simulated cognitive recovery is driven by massive synaptic 403 reconfiguration within low-gradient sensorimotor networks. This actively recruits structurally intact lower-404 order regions as an energy reservoir to drive global cortical re-awakening, effectively compensating for the 405 inertia of damaged higher-order hubs (e.g., the default mode network) 11,26 . Concurrently, to maintain 406 homeostatic stability within the pathological transmodal core, high-gradient regions exhibited a distinct 407 mechanistic profile that profound background suppression alongside a compensatory increase in inhibition. 408 Ultimately, this hierarchical reorganization provides a novel theoretical framework for understanding how 409 focal stimulation induces global functional recovery, highlighting that effective neuromodulation relies on 410 the coordinated rebalancing of E-I ratios across the entire cortical hierarchy 47,48 .411

## 4. Methods 432 

4.1 Multimodal neuroimaging datasets and preprocessing 433 

Participants 434 

This study utilized three independent cohorts, with all protocols approved by institutional ethics 435 committees and informed consent obtained. The healthy cohort consisted of 25 healthy young adults (13 436 females, age 24.2 ± 1.2) from the University of Electronic Science and Technology of China. The Alzheimer's 437 disease (AD) clinical cohort, sourced from Shenzhen People's Hospital, included 17 patients (14 females, age 438 65.5 ± 9.5) and 14 age-matched healthy controls (HCs, 12 females, age 56.4 ± 5.3; Tables S5-S6). Finally, 439 the mild cognitive impairment (MCI) clinical cohort, recruited from the Fourth People’s Hospital of Chengdu, 440 comprised 15 patients (8 females, age 64.7 ± 5.2) and 16 age-matched HCs (12 females, age 62.6 ± 5.5; 441 Tables S7-S8). 442 

MRI acquisition 443 

Structural (T1w), diffusion-weighted imaging (DWI), and resting-state functional MRI (rs-fMRI) data 444 were acquired using 3T scanners (GE Discovery MR750 for the healthy cohort; Siemens Skyra for both 445 clinical cohorts). High-resolution T1w images were acquired using standard 3D MPRAGE or IR-GRE 446 sequences (voxel size=1×1×1 mm³). DWI data were collected using single-shot EPI sequences with 64 447 diffusion directions (voxel size =2×2×4 mm³). The rs-fMRI data were acquired using gradient-echo EPI 448 sequences with a temporal resolution (TR) of approximately 2000 ms, capturing over 255 volumes for the 449 healthy cohort and 238 volumes for the clinical cohorts per participant. 450

Recordings utilized ASA-Lab amplifiers (ANT Neuro, Netherlands) for the 453 healthy cohort and LiveAmp 64 systems (Brain Products, Germany) for the clinical cohorts. The sampling 454 rates were 1000 Hz or 5000 Hz, which were subsequently downsampled during preprocessing. Uniquely, the 455 AD group underwent a 30-min session of repetitive transcranial magnetic stimulation (rTMS) targeting the 456 left dorsolateral prefrontal cortex (DLPFC), and then the EEG data were acquired immediately before 457 (RECpre) and after (RECpost) the intervention. 458 

Data Preprocessing 459 

We utilized three distinct brain atlases at 1 mm voxel resolution, including the Schaefer 200 and 500 460 cortical parcellations 54 , and the Brainnetome 246 atlas 55 , to define regions of interest (ROIs) for multimodal 461 network and leadfields construction. 462 

Structural connectivity (SC) 463 

SC matrices were derived from DWI data using the MRtrix3 toolbox 56 . Following standard denoising 464 and motion/bias field correction, images were registered to the MNI152 template using ANTs. Tissue 465 segmentation was performed using FSL 5ttgen for anatomically constrained tractography (ACT). Fiber 466 orientation distributions were estimated via constrained spherical deconvolution. Whole-brain probabilistic 467 tractography (iFOD2 with ACT) generated 200,000 streamlines per participant. SC matrices were constructed 468 by quantifying the normalized streamline counts connecting each pair of ROIs. 469 

fMRI BOLD signals 470 

The rs-fMRI data preprocessing was conducted using DPABI 57 . The fMRI images were slice-time 471 corrected, spatially realigned, normalized to MNI space (3×3×3 mm 3), and smoothed with a 6 mm full-width 472 half maximum (FWHM) Gaussian kernel. The nuisance signals, including 24 motion parameters and mean 473 white matter and cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) signals, were regressed out. The resulting BOLD time series were 474 bandpass filtered (0.01–0.1 Hz) and z-score normalized for each ROI. 475 

Functional connectivity (FC) 476 

For fMRI, FC matrices were constructed by computing Pearson correlation coefficients between all pairs 477 of regional BOLD time series, followed by Fisher's z-transformation to improve normality. For EEG, sensor-478 level FC was quantified across filter frequency bands (delta to gamma) using three complementary metrics 479 to capture diverse interaction mechanisms: (1) Amplitude envelope correlation (AEC) measures the 480 correlation of orthogonalized amplitude envelopes; (2) Magnitude-squared coherence quantifies the linear 481 dependence between two signals in the frequency domain; and (3) Phase-locking value (PLV) assesses the 482 consistency of instantaneous phase differences independent of signal amplitude. 483 

EEG signals 484 

EEG data were preprocessed using EEGLAB toolbox 58 . Following the removal of non-scalp channels 485 and the rejection of bad segments, signals were bandpass filtered (0.5–45 Hz) using a standard finite impulse 486 response (FIR) filter with zero-phase delay. The data were re-referenced to the Cz electrode, downsampled 487 to 250 Hz for computational efficiency. Independent component analysis (ICA) was applied to identify and 488 eliminate ocular, muscular, and cardiac artifacts and then z-score normalized for each channel. 489 

Individualized FEM-based forward modeling 490 

Individualized EEG forward modeling used the SimNIBS 4.1.0 to generate the realistic volume 491 conductor model 59 . High-quality individualized head meshes were reconstructed from T1w of each 492

where 𝐴 (𝑡 ) is the amplitude envelope, and 𝜙 (𝑡 ) is the instantaneous phase. Network synchrony was 603 quantified as the temporal mean of the 𝑅 (𝑡 ), reflecting the overall degree of phase alignment across the 604 network over time. Metastability was defined as the standard deviation of the 𝑅 (𝑡 ), indexing the temporal 605 variability of this synchronization, thereby capturing the tendency of system to continuously explore diverse 606 coordination states. 607 

Power spectral density 608 

The power spectral density (PSD) of the EEG signals (resampled to 250 Hz) was estimated utilizing 609 Welch’s method. Signals were segmented into 2-second epochs with 50% overlap and tapered with a 610 Hamming window to minimize spectral leakage. The resulting PSDs were averaged across epochs within the 611 0.5–45 Hz range for both empirical and simulated data. 612 

EEG microstates 613 

EEG microstates were extracted to analyze large-scale spatiotemporal dynamics 67 . Following standard 614 preprocessing, aggregated empirical global field power peaks were clustered into four canonical templates 615 using modified K-means. Individual continuous EEG data were then back-fitted to these templates (with 30-616 ms smoothing) to compute standard microstate characteristics (global explained variance, coverage, 617 occurrence, duration, and transition probabilities). For comparison, the identically preprocessed simulated 618 EEG signals were directly back-projected onto the empirically derived templates. 619 

Statistical analysis 620 

All correlations were evaluated using the Pearson correlation coefficient ( r). Differences between 621 empirical and simulated feature distributions, or between distinct clinical groups/conditions, were assessed 622 using paired or two-sample tests as appropriate. Normality was evaluated using the Shapiro-Wilk test. 623 Normally distributed data were analyzed using paired or independent t-tests, whereas non-parametric 624 equivalents (Wilcoxon signed-rank test or Mann-Whitney U test) were applied otherwise. The results were 625 considered significant at * p < 0.05, ** p < 0.01 and *** p < 0.001. Effect sizes were reported by Cohen’s d. 626
