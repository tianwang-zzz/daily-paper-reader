Abstract 33 

34 

INTRODUCTION: Locus coeruleus and glymphatic dysfunction are linked both to 35 

Alzheimer's disease (AD) and, recently, to infraslow oscillation in sleep spindle (sigma) 36 

activity (ISO). Here we hypothesise ISO integrity is a critical link between sleep and 37 

AD. 38 

METHODS: We analyzed non -rapid eye movement sleep EEG from AD and controls, 39 

extracting ISO peak amplitude, intrinsic frequency, and bandwidth from the sigma -40 

power time course. We assessed group differences and correlations with plasma 41 

biomarkers (Aβ42/40, pTau181 and 217, NfL, GFAP). 42 

RESULTS: ISO peak amplitude was significantly reduced in AD, while intrinsic 43 

frequency and bandwidth were preserved. ISO peak amplitude positively correlated 44 

with Aβ42/40 ratio, and ISO bandwidth correlated with GFAP and NfL levels, and with 45 

lower verbal memory retention. 46 

DISCUSSION: Such selective weakening of ISO in AD is consistent with LC 47 

dysfunction and impaired glymphatic cycling. ISO may be a novel mechanism and 48 

electrophysiological marker linking sleep microarchitecture to AD pathology. 49

INTRODUCTION 54 

55 

Sleep architecture is profoundly altered in Alzheimer’s disease (AD), and growing 56 

evidence suggests that these disruptions are not merely symptomatic but 57 

mechanistically linked to neurodegenerative processes  [1 –3] . In particular, non -rapid 58 

eye movement (NREM) sleep, during which coordinated oscillatory activity supports 59 

memory consolidation, synaptic homeostasis, and glymphatic clearance, markedly 60 

deteriorates early in the disease course  [4 –6] . Among the key electrophysiological 61 

markers of NREM sleep, spindle -related sigma (12 –16 Hz) activity has emerged as a 62 

sensitive indicator of thalamocortical circuit integrity and cognitive function  [7,8] .63 

Recent work has revealed that NREM sigma power is not constant but manifests 64 

infraslow oscillation (ISO), such that sleep spindles temporally cluster at <0.1 Hz 65 

[9,10] .66 

A growing body of evidence indicates that these ISO dynamics are tightly coupled to 67 

oscillatory activity in the locus coeruleus (LC) and brain release of noradrenaline (NA) 68 

[10 –12] . The LC exhibits rhythmic fluctuations in firing at ISO timescales, which 69 

produce corresponding modulations of NA release resulting in fluctuation of cortical 70 

arousal and sleep spindle expression during NREM sleep. 71 

Furthermore, these oscillatory fluctuations in noradrenergic activity during NREM 72 

sleep modulate pericyte contraction, astrocytic end -feet geometry, and vascular tone, 73 

thereby promoting cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) influx and metabolic waste clearance, 74 

including β -amyloid  [13,14] .75

The LC is among the earliest sites of tau -related pathology in AD, and impaired 76 

glymphatic function has been proposed as a key contributor to β -amyloid accumulation 77 

[15 –17] . Thus, disruptions in LC -driven infraslow rhythms may mechanistically link 78 

sleep disturbances to the pathological protein deposition characteristic of AD. 79 

In this study, we investigated the integrity of the ISO of sigma power in individuals with 80 

AD compared with age -matched healthy control participants. Here, we test whether 81 

breakdown of ISO coordination is evident in people affected by AD, reflecting LC 82 

dysfunction and impaired glymphatic cycling. We hypothesised that the integrity of the 83 

ISO of sigma power in individuals with AD would be reduced compared with age -84 

matched healthy control participants. 85

MATERIALS AND METHODS 87 

88 

Participants 89 

Thirty older adults (age > 50 years) were selected from the RESTED -AD cohort  [18] ,90 

a longitudinal observational study of sleep physiology in individuals with and without 91 

AD pathology. The sample included ten participants with AD and twenty cognitively 92 

healthy, age -matched controls. All participants met the RESTED -AD inclusion criteria 93 

and provided written informed consent. Details on recruitment, ethics, diagnostic 94 

procedures, and multimodal phenotyping have been described previously  [18] .95 

Protocol and procedures 96 

RESTED -AD is a naturalistic, home -based study designed to capture variability in 97 

sleep and behavior under real -world conditions. Following baseline clinical and 98 

neuropsychological assessment, participants underwent an intensive monitoring 99 

phase. Sleep was recorded for seven consecutive nights at home using a wireless 100 

multichannel portable EEG device (Dreem -2), with recordings self -initiated at habitual 101 

bedtime. 102 

Cognitive performance was assessed using a word list memory task including 103 

immediate and next -morning recall after evening learning session. Blood samples 104 

were collected according to standardized procedures for quantification of plasma 105 

biomarkers, including Aβ42 and Aβ40, phosphorylated tau species (pTau -181 and 106 

pTau -217), neurofilament light chain (NfL), and glial fibrillary acidic protein (GFAP). 107 

EEG preprocessing and selection of NREM2 segments 108

All EEG preprocessing and analyses were performed in MATLAB using custom scripts 109 

together with FieldTrip and EEGLAB (2021). EEG data were band -pass filtered 110 

between 0.5 and 35 Hz using zero -phase FIR filtering. Artifacts were identified using 111 

an automated epoch -based rejection procedure based on kurtosis and joint probability. 112 

Contiguous artifact -free stage 2 NREM (NREM2) segments of at least 5 minutes were 113 

selected, and fixed -length 5 -minute epochs were extracted for analysis. A frontal –114 

central signal of interest was obtained by averaging the two available bipolar 115 

derivations, approximating centro -parietal regions where sigma activity is maximal. 116 

Sleep staging and quality control 117 

Sleep staging was derived from automated hypnogram files generated by the portable 118 

EEG system. The Dreem algorithm, validated against expert -scored 119 

polysomnography, shows good agreement for sleep stage classification ( ∼83 –84% 120 

accuracy)  [19] . To minimize misclassification, all artifact -free NREM2 segments were 121 

visually inspected using a custom interface. Segments with residual artifacts or stage -122 

inconsistent EEG features were excluded. Only NREM2 segments independently 123 

validated by two expert scorers were retained for analysis, ensuring reliable infraslow 124 

measurements. 125 

Data post processing and analyses 126 

Isolation of ISO 127 

ISO dynamics were characterized following established procedures described in  [9] 128 

and extended in recent human studies  [20] . Continuous sigma -band amplitude time 129 

courses were derived using time -frequency decomposition, and their power spectra 130 

were analyzed in the infraslow range (<0.1 Hz) to quantify rhythmic modulation of 131

wavelets over frequencies from 10 to 16 Hz in 0.2 Hz steps). The amplitude envelope 133 

was obtained by computing the absolute value of the complex Fourier coefficients and 134 

averaging it across frequencies at 0.1 second steps (10 Hz sampling). Time courses 135 

were detrended, demeaned, and smoothed using a moving -average filter (5 s window) 136 

to reduce activity unrelated to infraslow dynamics. Power spectral density of the sigma 137 

envelope was then computed using Welch’s method (window type:Hanning, 240s with 138 

90% overlap), retaining spectral estimates in the 0 –0.12 Hz range. To facilitate 139 

comparisons across nights and individuals, spectra were expressed in relative units 140 

by normalizing to median infraslow power and averaged across accepted segments 141 

within each night. 142 

ISO feature extraction and quality control 143 

ISO features were extracted from relative power spectra using a model -based fitting 144 

approach. For each spectrum, a three -parameter (frequency, amplitude, width) 145 

Gaussian function with an additive constant term (constant baseline) was fitted. This 146 

formulation explicitly models the infraslow spectral peak as a localized oscillatory 147 

component superimposed on a non -oscillatory spectral background. Although spectra 148 

were expressed in relative units to account for variability in overall signal amplitude, 149 

retaining a baseline term allows residual broadband structure in the infra -slow range 150 

to be estimated directly from the data. This method avoids a priori assumptions about 151 

baseline shape (e.g., flat or predefined offsets) and accounts for aperiodic (1/f -like) 152 

components of EEG power spectra  [21,22] . Fits were restricted to the 0.01 –0.04 Hz 153 

range, previously shown to capture dominant ISO modulation of sigma power  [9] . ISO 154 

peak frequency, peak amplitude, and bandwidth were defined from the Gaussian fit as 155

Power analysis 162 

EEG spectral power was quantified during NREM2 and NREM3 using pre -processed 163 

data segments. Continuous NREM data were divided into non -overlapping 5 -s epochs, 164 

and power spectra were estimated using a multitaper FFT approach over the 0.5 -35 165 

Hz range (using FieldTrip with a multitaper FFT approach with spectral smoothing of 166 

2 Hz, zero -padding to the epoch length, and linear detrending per epoch; frequency 167 

range 0.5 -35 Hz). Band -limited power was computed for delta (0.5 –4 Hz; SWA), alpha 168 

(8 –12 Hz), sigma (12 –16 Hz), and beta (18 –35 Hz). Total broadband power was 169 

defined as the integrated power over 0.5 -35 Hz. Relative power was expressed as the 170 

percentage contribution of each band to total broadband power. Epoch -level power 171 

estimates were averaged within each sleep stage and night, and subsequently across 172 

nights to obtain subject -level mean power values for NREM2 and NREM3. Relative 173 

power measures were interpreted as reflecting the spectral composition of NREM 174 

activity rather than absolute signal amplitude. 175

RESULTS 

Dataset quality description 

After quality control, one participant was excluded due to poor EEG data quality. A 

total of ~2,050 artefact -free 5 -minute NREM2 segments (~170 h of NREM sleep) were 

retained across 152 nights from 29 participants. Nights with fewer than two valid 

segments (10.53%) or without a dominant infra -slow oscillatory peak (7.9%, see 

methods section) were excluded, resulting in a final sample of 124 nights (AD: 41 

nights from 9 participants; controls: 83 nights from 18 participants). No differences 

were observed bet ween groups in the number of nights included per subject (P = 0.95) 

or in the average number of valid NREM2 segments per night (P = 0.21; 

Supplementary Figure 1). 

ISO of the sigma power is reduced in AD 

We quantified the modulation of ISO by extracting three features from each 

participant’s ISO spectrum of the sigma power time course: (i) the ISO peak amplitude, 

reflecting the strength of the rhythmic oscillation; (ii) the ISO intrinsic frequency, 

represe nting the dominant oscillatory rate within the infraslow range; and (iii) the ISO 

bandwidth, capturing the spread of spectral power around the peak frequency. Across 

groups, a clear reduction emerged in the ISO peak amplitude in AD patients compared 

with h ealthy controls ( -28,03 ± 6.88 %, P = 0.003, Figure 1), indicating a marked 

weakening of the infraslow oscillation amplitude of sigma activity. In contrast, neither 

the ISO intrinsic frequency (P = 0.77) nor the ISO bandwidth (P = 0.79) differed 

significan tly between groups (Figure 1), suggesting that while the rhythmic structure 

and frequency of the oscillation are preserved, its overall strength is selectively

ISO features correlate with plasma biomarkers of disease 

We next assessed whether alterations in infraslow oscillation features were related to 

established plasma biomarkers of AD pathology and neurodegeneration. A significant 

positive correlation emerged between ISO peak amplitude and the Aβ42/40 ratio 

(Table 1 ), such that individuals with greater infraslow oscillation tended to exhibit more 

favorable amyloid profiles. Notably, AD patients clustered at the lower end of both ISO 

peak amplitude and Aβ42/40 ratio (Figure 2A), consistent with a coupling between 

dimi nished infraslow oscillation and greater amyloid burden. 

In addition, we observed that the ISO bandwidth showed positive correlations with 

plasma levels of GFAP and NfL (across AD and control groups), markers of astroglial 

activation and axonal injury, respectively (Table 1). Participants with broader infraslow 

spectral bandwidth tended to present with higher concentrations of these biomarkers 

(Figure 2B-C), suggesting that more heterogeneity in spectral features of infraslow 

activity may reflect underlying glial and neurodegenerative processes. No significant 

as sociations were found for the ISO intrinsic frequency (Table 1).

DISCUSSION 

This study examines whether alterations in ISO dynamics, previously proposed to 

reflect LC activity and influence glymphatic function, are detectable in human sleep 

and relate to molecular markers of AD pathology. By quantifying ISO features from 

overnight EEG, we demonstrate a selective reduction in ISO peak amplitude in people 

with clinically -diagnosed AD, while intrinsic frequency and bandwidth remain 

preserved. This pattern reveals a specific weakening of the rhythmic modulation that 

structures sigma ac tivity, suggesting impaired LC -driven arousal cycling during NREM 

sleep. 

Our findings offer a novel perspective by showing that ISO alterations are not only 

present in AD but are meaningfully related to plasma biomarkers of amyloid 

dysregulation and neurodegeneration. The positive association between ISO 

amplitude and Aβ42/40 ratio indicates that preserved infraslow modulation 

accompanies a more favorable amyloid profile, while correlations between ISO 

bandwidth and GFAP and NfL suggest that additional spectral properties track glial 

activation and axonal injury. Specifically, b roader ISO bandwidth corresponds to a less 

sharply defined infraslow oscillatory peak and greater dispersion of power across 

frequencies, a pattern consistent with reduced ISO stability. No significant 

associations were found for the ISO intrinsic frequenc y. In parallel, the association 

between broader ISO bandwidth and poorer word retention provides initial evidence 

that alterations in infraslow sigma modulation may also relate to sleep -dependent 

memory processes impaired in AD  [7,24] . Finally, ISO features did not correlate with 

delta power, the canonical electrophysiological marker of sleep depth frequently 

altered in AD  [23,25] . This dissociation suggests that ISO abnormalities are not driven

with recent observations showing that NREM noradrenergic oscillations are more

Several limitations should be acknowledged. The study is cross -sectional, preventing 

direct inference about temporal ordering between ISO disruption and biomarker 

changes. LC function and glymphatic activity were not measured directly, and EEG -

derived ISO features represent downstream proxies rather than mechanistic readouts. 

Sample size and technical factors with recording device (such as bipolar derivations 

or non -standard electrode positions) may limit detection of delta or spindle power and 

subtler asso ciations. 

Despite these limitations, the present findings have clear implications. They suggest 

that reduced ISO amplitude may reflect a dysfunction of LC -glymphatic regulatory 

systems, offering a testable framework for understanding how sleep microarchitecture 

cont ributes to AD progression. Specific next steps will include: (1) longitudinal 

evaluation to determine whether ISO amplitude predicts cognitive decline or biomarker 

trajectories; (2) integration with LC imaging (neuromelanin MRI or noradrenergic PET) 

to dir ectly link ISO alterations to LC integrity; and (3) intervention studies targeting 

noradrenergic tone or arousal stability to assess whether ISO amplitude can be 

restored and whether such restoration enhances glymphatic function or reduces 

pathological pro tein burden. 

In conclusion, this work identifies weakened infraslow modulation of sigma activity as 

a potential electrophysiological signature of AD -related network and clearance 

dysfunction. Establishing ISO features as accessible, biologically grounded
