Abstract 

Adapting LLMs to new domains causes forgetting because standard methods (full fine-tuning, LoRA) inject new directions into the weight space. We propose GAIN , which re-emphasizes 

existing features through multiplicative modulation Wnew = S · W . The learned diagonal matrix 

S is applied to the attention output projection and optionally the FFN. The principle mirrors 

gain modulation in neuroscience, where neurons adapt to context by scaling response strength while preserving selectivity. We evaluate GAIN on five models from four families (774M to 70B), adapting sequentially across eight domains. GAIN-FFN matches LoRA’s in-domain adaptation, but their effects on previously trained domains are opposite: GAIN-FFN improves them by 7–13% (validation PPL), while LoRA degrades them by 18 –36% . Downstream accuracy confirms the pattern: for example, after seven sequential adaptations on Qwen2.5, GAIN-FFN degrades BoolQ by only 0.8% while LoRA damages it by 14 .9% . GAIN adds 46K–230K parameters per model and can be absorbed into the pretrained weights for zero inference cost. 

# 1 Introduction 

Large language models are powerful general-purpose systems but underperform on specialized domains, motivating adaptation. Full fine-tuning updates all parameters and is well known to forget catastrophically (Kirkpatrick et al., 2017). LoRA (Hu et al., 2022) is a parameter-efficient alternative that updates far fewer parameters and reduces forgetting. But forgetting remains a significant problem—Biderman et al. (2024b) trace it to “intruder dimensions”: new directions injected into the weight space that interfere with pretrained capabilities. Specifically, LoRA adds a low-rank perturbation to the weight matrix ( Wnew = W + BA ), and the perturbation BA can introduce directions outside the pretrained output space. We propose to prevent intruder dimensions during domain adaptation. Since pretrained models are trained on diverse corpora and already encode features for many domains, adaptation can 

re-emphasize existing features rather than adding new ones. Our method, GAIN , scales the weight matrix directly: 

Wnew = S · W (1) where S is a diagonal matrix that scales rows of W . The output directions of the adapted weight are unchanged—the adapted model can only use features that already exist in the pretrained weights (Proposition 1). This principle is inspired by gain modulation in neuroscience (Salinas and 1

> arXiv:2604.04516v1 [cs.LG] 6 Apr 2026

Thier, 2000; Andersen and Mountcastle, 1985): neurons adjust response amplitude based on context without changing what they respond to (Section 3). GAIN applies this principle to the attention output projection and optionally the FFN (Section 2). We evaluate on five models from four families (774M to 70B) and eight domains, with three key findings: 1. Structural forgetting prevention. GAIN adapts with near-zero forgetting at every learning rate. Both backward forgetting (damage to earlier-trained domains) and forward interference (damage to not-yet-trained domains) arise from additive methods introducing directions outside the pretrained output space; GAIN prevents both by construction. 2. Positive transfer under sequential adaptation. Across eight sequential domains and five models, GAIN-FFN improves previously trained domains by 7–13% (validation PPL), while LoRA degrades them by 18 –36% . Downstream accuracy confirms: on Qwen2.5, GAIN-FFN degrades BoolQ by only 0.8% while LoRA damages it by 14 .9% .3. No forgetting–adaptation tradeoff. LoRA can reduce forgetting via L2 regularization or conservative learning rates, but only by sacrificing in-domain adaptation—and these techniques require domain boundaries. GAIN achieves strong adaptation with near-zero forgetting simultaneously, requiring no regularization and no domain boundaries. 

# 2 Methods 

We propose two methods: GAIN scales the attention output projection, and GAIN-FFN extends this to the FFN. 

2.1 GAIN 

We instantiate Eq. 1 for the attention output projection WO. A transformer with L layers and H

heads computes: output = [ head 1, . . . , head H ] WO (2) where WO ∈ Rdmodel ×dmodel projects concatenated head outputs into the residual stream. GAIN inserts a diagonal scaling matrix before WO:output = [ head 1, . . . , head H ] S W O, S = diag (s1, . . . , s dmodel ) (3) Each si is a learned scaling factor. This gives dmodel parameters per layer, dmodel × L total. For example, with GPT-2 Large ( dmodel = 1280 , L = 36 ), this is 46,080 parameters. We freeze all pretrained weights and initialize S = I, so that training starts from the pretrained model. After training, S is absorbed into the weights: WO ← S · WO, adding zero inference cost.

# 3 Multiplicative Modulation 

This section discusses why multiplicative modulation preserves pretrained capabilities while additive methods do not, and traces the connection to gain modulation in neuroscience. In transformers, each layer’s output is added to a shared hidden state (the residual stream). The output projection WO determines what each attention layer contributes. LoRA modifies WO

additively: LoRA: Wnew = WO + BA (additive) (6) The perturbation BA is independent of WO, so for input h, the output (WO + BA )h = WOh +

BAh includes BAh —a term that can introduce entirely new directions. GAIN modifies weights multiplicatively: GAIN: W new  

> O

= S · WO (7) GAIN-FFN: W new down = Wdown · Sffn (8) Both are instances of diagonal scaling (left and right multiplication, respectively). The following proposition shows that both forms preserve the pretrained output space. 

Proposition 1 (Output Space Preservation) . For diagonal S and weight matrix W :1. Left multiplication : rowspace (S · W ) ⊆ rowspace (W )

2. Right multiplication : colspan (W · S) ⊆ colspan (W )

That is, multiplicative modulation can only produce outputs within the pretrained model’s output subspace. Proof. (1) The i-th row of S · W is si · w⊤ 

> i

, a scaling of the i-th row of W , hence in rowspace (W ).(2) The j-th column of W · S is sj · wj , a scaling of the j-th column of W , hence in colspan (W ).The “ ⊆” becomes “ =” when all si > 0, since no row or column is zeroed out. In our experiments, learned scaling factors remain well above zero (e.g., smin = 0 .94 on GPT-2 at lr = 10 −3), so the 3stronger equality holds in practice. In contrast, LoRA does not preserve the pretrained output space: the perturbation BA is independent of W , so the output space of W + BA can escape that of W .Biderman et al. (2024b) show that LoRA introduces “intruder dimensions”—out-of-subspace singular vectors causally linked to forgetting. Chen et al. (2024) show that LoRA minima can be sharp in full parameter space. GAIN avoids the first issue: Proposition 1 prevents intruder dimensions by construction. Since it holds at every layer, the guarantee composes through depth; for LoRA, intruder dimensions at early layers compound through subsequent layers. The second issue is also mitigated, since the diagonal constraint restricts optimization to a narrow subspace anchored at the pretrained weights, reducing the chance of reaching sharp minima in full parameter space. 

3.1 Connection to Neuroscience 

GAIN’s multiplicative principle is inspired by neuroscience. Gain modulation (Treue and Martínez-Trujillo, 1999; Salinas and Thier, 2000; Salinas and Abbott, 2001) shows that when a neuron’s output is multiplicatively scaled, its selectivity—the features it responds to—is preserved; only the amplitude changes. Additive perturbation, by contrast, shifts selectivity. The connection goes beyond analogy. Take GAIN for example. The attention output can be rewritten as: 

h · (S · WO) = ( h · S) · WO (9) The left-hand side is the weight modification view (Eq. 1). The right-hand side offers a second perspective: GAIN does not modify WO at all—it scales the hidden activations h before they enter 

WO. Each diagonal entry si is a gain applied to the i-th dimension of h, amplifying or suppressing that dimension’s contribution. This mirrors gain modulation in neuroscience: context-dependent scaling of neural responses, with the downstream projection unchanged. GAIN-FFN has the same interpretation: (Wdown · Sffn ) · z = Wdown · (Sffn · z) scales the FFN intermediate activations z before the down-projection.

# 5 Sequential Multi-Domain Adaptation 

In practice, models are adapted to new domains over time—for example, a medical deployment followed by legal, then financial. Each new adaptation risks erasing earlier ones, a tension known as the stability-plasticity dilemma (Kirkpatrick et al., 2017). Continual learning methods address this with replay buffers or regularization. GAIN requires none of these mechanisms—its protection follows from the multiplicative structure itself (Proposition 1).

Why does positive transfer occur? GAIN not only avoids forgetting—it improves earlier domains (negative lower-triangle entries in Table 2). To understand the mechanism, we ask: do different domains learn similar scaling factors? We train GAIN-FFN independently on each of five domains (five separate models, each starting from the pretrained model) and compare the resulting scaling factors. Most of the adaptation is domain-specific: cross-domain correlations are near zero (r = 0 .00 –0.14 ). However, 20 –40% of the variance is shared across all domains. In this shared component, all five domains agree on which dimensions to suppress and which to amplify. We hypothesize that this shared component corrects pretrained feature weightings that are suboptimal for downstream text generally. Training on any domain applies this correction, benefiting all others. We verify this with a causal ablation. After training on each domain, we decompose the scaling update into its shared and domain-specific components. Removing the shared component halves positive transfer. Keeping only the shared component retains 81% of it. The shared component is thus both necessary and largely sufficient for positive transfer. # 6 Conclusion 

We propose GAIN, a multiplicative approach to domain adaptation that scales existing features rather than adding new directions. The theoretical basis is Proposition 1: diagonal scaling preserves the pretrained output space, preventing the intruder dimensions that cause forgetting in additive methods like LoRA. Across eight domains, five models from four families (774M to 70B), and multiple random orderings, GAIN-FFN improves earlier-trained domains by 7–13% while LoRA degrades them by 

18 –36% . On seven downstream benchmarks, GAIN-FFN preserves or improves accuracy while LoRA damages BoolQ by up to 14 .9% . The stability-plasticity tradeoff that governs additive methods does not apply: GAIN operates entirely in the positive-transfer regime. These results hold at 10 ×

larger data budgets, across learning rates, and with identical hyperparameters across all models. GAIN adds 46K–230K parameters per model, requires no domain boundaries or regularization, and can be absorbed into the pretrained weights for zero inference cost. Limitations include the assumption that pretrained features are sufficient for all target domains, and the lack of evaluation on instruction-tuned models or very long domain sequences ( ≫ 8).

ICLR , 2025. 10 A Related Work 

LoRA and variants (Hu et al., 2022; Dettmers et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2024) add low-rank perturbations to weight matrices. We show these additive methods live on a forgetting–adaptation tradeoff curve that multiplicative methods avoid. Biderman et al. (2024b) identify “intruder dimensions” in LoRA that interfere with pretrained capabilities—our Proposition 1 shows GAIN avoids these by construction. Chen et al. (2024) show that LoRA minima can be deceptively sharp in full parameter space, causing forgetting. (IA) 3 (Liu et al., 2022) scales keys, values, and FFN activations multiplicatively for few-shot classification. HiRA (Zhang et al., 2025) uses Hadamard products of pretrained weights with low-rank matrices. SVFT (Lingam et al., 2024) scales singular values of pretrained weights. PiCa (Wang et al., 2025) projects gradients onto the pretrained column space. All are multiplicative but none studies forgetting or sequential adaptation. We confirm that (IA) 3 also exhibits positive transfer in the sequential setting ( −14 .2% , comparable to GAIN-FFN’s −12 .9% ), validating that the multiplicative principle—not GAIN’s specific parameterization—is the key mechanism (Appendix L). Continual learning methods address forgetting through replay buffers (Rolnick et al., 2019), regularization (Kirkpatrick et al., 2017), or architecture partitioning (Mallya and Lazebnik, 2018). GAIN is not a continual learning method—its protection follows from the multiplicative structure (Proposition 1).

This confirms that the multiplicative principle (Proposition 1) is the key mechanism, not the specific choice of which weight matrices to scale. Both GAIN-FFN and (IA) 3 stay within the pretrained output space; both show positive transfer. The methods differ in which dimensions they scale (GAIN: WO and Wdown ; (IA) 3: keys, values, and FFN inputs), but the structural guarantee is the same. Neither is a special case of the other: GAIN scales the output projections (affecting what is produced ), while (IA) 3 scales inputs to the attention computation (affecting what is attended to ). The methods are complementary and could be combined. 19
