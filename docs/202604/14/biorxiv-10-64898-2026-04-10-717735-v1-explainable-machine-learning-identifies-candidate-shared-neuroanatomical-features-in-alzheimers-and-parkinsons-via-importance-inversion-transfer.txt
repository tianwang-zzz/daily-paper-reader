Abstract 

Despite significant neurobiological and pathological overlaps, Alzheimer’s (AD) and Parkinson’s (PD)—the primary threats to healthy aging—are still managed as distinct clinical entities. Standard machine learning exacerbates this frag-mentation by prioritizing divergent markers over shared traits, obscuring the invariant foundations of neurodegeneration. This study introduces an explainable framework leveraging Importance Inversion Transfer (IIT) to identify candi-date neuroanatomical features that show relative stability across both disorders. By prioritizing structural anchors invariant across the neurodegenerative spec-trum, IIT isolates candidate shared structural features. Analysis of multi-regional brain volumes identifies eight shared anchors, confirmed via an inductive valida-tion protocol with high diagnostic robustness (AUC = 0.894). Findings reveal a morphological continuum between healthy aging and neurodegeneration, sug-gesting the presence of partially shared structural substrates. These results

# 1 Introduction 1

Alzheimer’s disease (AD) and Parkinson’s disease (PD) constitute the foremost causes 2

of neurodegeneration in geriatric populations, imposing a mounting strain on global 3

healthcare infrastructure [1, 2]. As the most prevalent neurodegenerative conditions, 4

these disorders impact tens of millions worldwide; while AD manifests primarily 5

through progressive cognitive erosion and memory loss, PD is clinically distinguished 6

by hallmark motor impairments, including bradykinesia, tremor, and rigidity [3–5]. 7

Conventional clinical paradigms categorize these disorders as separate entities defined 8

by symptomatic silos. However, emerging evidence highlights substantial pathological 9

convergences at the molecular and cellular levels which frequently manifest decades 10 

prior to clinical onset [6–11]. 11 

These convergences have led to the hypothesis that AD and PD may share partially 12 

overlapping pathological mechanisms, potentially reflecting a common early vulnera-13 

bility phase. This perspective has been formalized as the Neurodegenerative Elderly 14 

Syndrome (NES) hypothesis [8, 12]. The NES framework delineates a three-phase 15 

progression: an initial seeding stage defined by subclinical αSyn and monoaminer-16 

gic failure; a compensatory stage wherein homeostatic regulation preserves functional 17 

integrity despite incipient decay; and a terminal bifurcation stage in which damage 18 

to specific neural circuits yields phenotype-specific manifestations. Despite this theo-19 

retical foundation, mapping the shared neuroanatomical backbone that sustains the 20 

syndrome prior to clinical divergence remains a critical challenge in geriatric neurology. 21 

Exploring the plausibility of the NES hypothesis requires analytical methods 22 

capable of capturing both shared and divergent features within complex, multimodal 23 

datasets. While machine learning (ML) offers powerful pattern detection capabilities, 24 

clinical application demands transparency to ensure safety and reliability. Explainable 25 

machine learning (XML) addresses this requirement by identifying the specific features 26 

driving model predictions, thereby enabling a mechanistic interpretation of neurode-27 

generative trajectories. Recent literature underscores the versatility of XML in this 28 

domain, spanning from broad applications in diagnosis and treatment monitoring [13] 29 

to the identification of manifestations through digital phenotyping [10] and the opti-30 

mization of multi-target drug combinations [14]. Specifically, XML has proved pivotal 31 

in unraveling sex-specific pathological signatures in both PD [15] and AD, facilitating 32 

the development of clinician-tailored diagnostic interfaces [16]. However, despite these 33

## 3.2 Compensatory Latency and Phenotypic Bifurcation 237 

High-accuracy binary classification ( AU C = 0 .981, Table 2) suggests that AD and PD 238 

occupy mutually exclusive morphometric manifolds during advanced disease stages. 239 

Yet, the global 3-way task reveals a critical asymmetry: while the AD signature remains 240 

highly distinct (88 .6% accuracy), the significant misclassification of PD subjects as 241 

Healthy Controls (33 .3%) indicates the morphological continuum underlying early 242 

neurodegeneration (Fig. 1). This statistical proximity is consistent with the proposed 243 

compensatory stage within the NES framework, wherein homeostatic mechanisms 244 

effectively mask structural decay within the variance inherent in physiological aging. 245 

As discussed in the context of antagonistic trajectories (cf., Section 3.1), the 246 

observed “negative atrophy” in the PD brain stem and cerebral white matter highlights 247 

the macrostructural footprint of reactive plasticity or neuro-inflammatory upregu-248 

lation. This hypertrophic signature suggests a systemic effort to maintain network 249 

stability, potentially driven by the upregulation of noradrenergic and serotonergic 250 

systems in response to early dopaminergic depletion [8]. 251 

The pronounced contrast between this structural resilience in PD and the aggres-252 

sive limbic collapse characterizing AD necessitates the application of IIT logic. By 253 

isolating invariant structural anchors that persist across the AD–PD transition, the 254 

framework captures latent pathological signatures well before clinical phenotypes 255 

achieve diagnostic maturity. This non-linear progression, where compensatory remod-256 

eling delays the emergence of hallmark symptoms, supports homeostatic markers as 257 

primary diagnostic indicators capable of bypassing the classificatory noise inherent in 258 

the bifurcation stage. 259

## 3.3 Anatomical Divergence and the NES Core Hierarchy 260 

The quantitative divergence between Borda-driven separators and IIT-driven anchors 261 

delineates the structural transition from shared pathological seeding to phenotypic 262 

bifurcation (Figures 3, 4). Unlike traditional diagnostic paradigms prioritizing the Hip-263 

pocampus for classification accuracy, IIT isolates the RH Choroid Plexus and the LH 264 

Transverse Temporal cortex as invariant master anchors. Statistical indistinguisha-265 

bility and absolute structural consistency within these nodes may reflect an early 266 

anatomical locus of shared vulnerability, consistent with the NES seeding stage [8] 267 

(Figure 6, Table 3). 268 

Morphological symmetry in the LH Transverse Temporal cortex may be compati-269 

ble with alterations in neuromodulatory systems (e.g., noradrenergic and serotonergic 270 

pathways), although this interpretation cannot be directly tested with the present 271 

data. As the site of the primary auditory cortex (Heschl’s gyrus), this region relies 272 

on dense projections from the Locus Coeruleus and the Raphe Nuclei to maintain 273 

cortical arousal and auditory signal-to-noise modulation [8, 48–50]. Volumetric analy-274 

sis identifies a hemispheric gradient within this manifold: significant structural decay 275 

characterizes the right hemisphere ( p = 0 .0025), while the left remains statistically 276 

contiguous with the healthy baseline ( p = 0.0606). This lateralized vulnerability 277 

suggests that sensory hub degradation precedes the collapse of associative networks. 278 

Simultaneously, bilateral Choroid Plexus hypertrophy is consistent with the 279 

possibility of altered system-level homeostatic regulation. As the primary site for cere-280 

brospinal fluid (CSF) production and glymphatic waste clearance, invariant Choroid 281 

Plexus expansion suggests that impaired proteostatic drainage precedes the accumu-282 

lation of disease-specific aggregates [51, 52]. The RH Choroid Plexus (p = 0 .051) 283 

represents a node of maximum convergence within a context of brain-wide divergence. 284 

While the magnitude of this hit is more pronounced in AD ( p < 0.001), consistent with 285 

[53], the underlying attempt to manage proteinopathic overload through increased 286 

CSF production remains synchronized across phenotypes. 287

## 3.4 Etiological Drivers of the Neuroanatomical Backbone 346 

Identification of the RH Choroid Plexus and LH Transverse Temporal cortex as mas-347 

ter anchors aligns with the shared risk profile characteristic of the NES seeding stage 348 

[8]. Common environmental triggers, including high iron intake and pesticide expo-349 

sure, drive this invariant structural backbone by inducing early homeostatic failure 350 

and proteostatic stress [59, 60]. Specifically, expansion of the choroid plexus across 351 

both cohorts suggests a shared response to impaired waste clearance, potentially exac-352 

erbated by systemic factors such as high cholesterol, which compromise blood-CSF 353 

barrier integrity in both phenotypes [61]. 354 

Conversely, etiological factors with divergent risk profiles modulate the transition 355 

to the bifurcation stage—captured through the inductive assessment of discrimina-356 

tive features. Agents such as nicotine and coffee exert antagonistic effects, offering 357 

protection for the PD trajectory while potentially increasing vulnerability in the AD 358 

path [8, 62]. This etiological divergence mirrors the behavior of the identified Transi-359 

tion Pillars (e.g., Amygdala and Hippocampus), which maintain absolute rank-order 360 

consistency ( ρ = 1 .0) despite significant divergence in atrophy magnitude. These 361 

findings suggest the possibility that while shared genetic and environmental factors 362 

may contribute to an invariant neuroanatomical core, subsequent interactions with 363 

lifestyle-related variables could play a role in the bifurcation into divergent clini-364 

cal manifestations. This is consistent with the NES hypothesis that AD and PD 365 

potentially share early structural features before diverging. 366 

# 4 Conclusion 367 

This study introduces a framework for identifying candidate shared neuroanatomi-368 

cal features across neurodegenerative conditions. Implementation of IIT logic suggests 369 

that a stable set of structural anchors remains invariant across the neurodegener-370 

ative spectrum, notwithstanding divergent symptomatic expressions. Identification 371 

of this unified backbone yields quantitative preliminary empirical support for the 372 

NES hypothesis, facilitating a perspective shift from phenotype-specific silos toward 373 

a holistic, system-level view on brain aging [19]. 374 

These insights carry profound implications for geriatric neurology and biomarker 375 

discovery. Isolation of the invariant pathological core pinpoint anatomical landmarks 376 

critical to the subclinical seeding and compensatory stages of neurodegeneration. 377 

By shifting the analytical objective from maximal group separation to structural 378 

commonality, this methodology provides a robust foundation for early intervention 379 

and cross-disciplinary knowledge propagation. Ultimately, this approach transforms 380 

machine learning from a descriptive classifier into a discovery engine capable of 381 

elucidating universal structural laws governing complex neurodegenerative processes. 382 

While the identified backbone appears robust, several limitations should be consid-383 

ered. First, although the analysis incorporates longitudinal information through the 384

# 5 Methods 397 

## 5.1 Cohort Selection and Data Acquisition 398 

Data originate from two large-scale repositories: the Alzheimer’s Disease Neuroimaging 399 

Initiative (ADNI) 1 and the Parkinson’s Progression Markers Initiative (PPMI) 2. ADNI 400 

tracks neurodegenerative progression from mild cognitive impairment to AD through 401 

integrated longitudinal markers, while PPMI comprises a comprehensive multi-modal 402 

dataset spanning idiopathic and genetic PD cases alongside healthy controls. 403 

The finalized cohort includes 303 individuals distributed equally across three 404 

experimental groups: Healthy Controls (CN, n = 101), AD ( n = 101), and PD 405 

(n = 101). Strict matching for age, sex, and total intracranial volume ensures cohort 406 

comparability and minimizes demographic confounding. 407 

## 5.2 MRI Acquisition and Image Processing 408 

High-resolution T1-weighted sequences provide the foundation for precise morphomet-409 

ric assessment. The dataset utilizes two optimized protocols: MPRAGE and MPRAGE 410 

GRAPPA , which leverages parallel imaging to reduce scan time without compromis-411 

ing image fidelity [65, 66]. Technical specifications across platforms are standardized 412 

to ensure cross-cohort homogeneity (Table 4). 413 

The FastSurfer pipeline, a GPU-accelerated implementation of FreeSurfer, executes 414 

automated segmentation and parcellation [66]. This framework integrates HypVINN 415 

and CerebNet modules to extend traditional parcellation to hypothalamic and cere-416 

bellar structures [67, 68]. Preprocessing incorporates motion correction, intensity 417 

normalization, and affine registration to the MNI305 coordinate space, followed by 418 

automated skull stripping and subcortical segmentation via atlas-based probabilistic 419 

models. Spherical registration maps cortical folding patterns to an atlas template, 420 

yielding a robust feature set encompassing gray matter volume, cortical thickness, and 421 

surface area across 84 regions. 422

## 5.3 Inductive Benchmarking and Model Generalization 433 

The evaluation of diagnostic robustness follows a dual-path strategy to bifurcate model 434 

benchmarking from structural discovery. The inductive path establishes clinical relia-435 

bility through three optimized architectures: Random Forest (RF), Gradient Boosting 436 

(GB), and Logistic Regression (LR). This selection encompasses both a regularized lin-437 

ear baseline (LR) and models capable of capturing high-order non-linear interactions 438 

(RF, GB). 439 

A stratified 5-fold cross-validation scheme, repeated across 10 independent stochas-440 

tic realizations, ensures that performance metrics remain uninflated by data leakage. 441 

Within each loop, all preprocessing—including median imputation for missing vol-442 

umes and Z-score standardization—is performed nested within the training fold. 443 

An automated GridSearchCV protocol executes hyperparameter optimization within 444 

these folds: RF utilizes 500 estimators with balanced class weights; GB incorpo-445 

rates stochastic subsampling (0.8); and LR employs an L2 penalty with optimized 446 

C coefficients. Evaluation prioritizes ROC-AUC, Accuracy, and F 1-macro to capture 447 

decision-boundary sensitivity and stability under cohort imbalance. 448

## 5.5 Dimensionality Control and Statistical Validation 470 

The reduction of 84 initial volumetric descriptors into ten stable invariants optimizes 471 

the effective subject-to-feature ratio from N/p ≈ 3 to N/p ≈ 30. This dimensionality 472
